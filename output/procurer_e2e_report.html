Procurer Supply Chain Optimization Report

1. Executive Summary

This report presents a comprehensive, data-driven analysis of supply chain procurement using one or more solver methods. The analysis includes realistic modeling of lead times, distinguishing between when orders are placed (procurement) and when they arrive (shipments). All input data, model logic, and results are fully transparent for technical and executive review.

Key Insights:

  • Procurement vs Shipments: Orders are placed in advance considering lead times, with shipments arriving later
  • Safety Stock Management: Minimum inventory levels are maintained throughout the planning horizon
  • Cost Optimization: Total cost includes procurement, logistics, and holding costs
  • Constraint Satisfaction: All demand must be met while respecting capacity and MOQ constraints

2. Solver Logic & Approach

Solvers Activated in This Report: Linear (MILP)

Linear Solver (MILP):

  • Finds the optimal procurement and inventory plan by minimizing total cost (procurement, logistics, holding).
  • Respects all constraints: demand fulfillment, inventory balance, warehouse capacity, safety stock, shelf life, and minimum order quantity (MOQ).
  • Models realistic lead times: orders placed in period t arrive in period t + lead_time.
  • Uses a mathematical programming solver to guarantee the best solution for the given data.
  • Distinguishes between procurement (order placement) and shipments (order arrival).

Nonlinear Solver (NLP with Discounts):

  • Similar to the linear solver, but models quantity discounts: if you buy more than a threshold, you get a lower price for the extra units.
  • Minimizes total cost, including the effect of discounts, using a nonlinear optimization approach.
  • Also models lead times and distinguishes procurement from shipments.

Heuristic Solver:

  • Works period by period, projecting inventory forward and ordering when safety stock is threatened.
  • Orders from the cheapest available supplier when projected inventory falls below safety stock.
  • Fast and simple, but may not find the absolute best solution.
  • Applies discounts greedily if order quantity exceeds the discount threshold.
  • Models lead times and distinguishes procurement from shipments.

3. Model Variables, Data & Assumptions

Key Concepts & Variables

üîë Understanding Procurement vs Shipments

This optimization model distinguishes between two critical moments in the supply chain timeline:

üìã Procurement Plan - When Orders Are Placed

Procurement is when you place an order with a supplier. This is when you pay and commit to purchasing.

  • Timing: Orders placed in advance, considering lead times
  • Financial Impact: Cash flows out when you place the order
  • Example: Need products in period 3, supplier has 2-period lead time ‚Üí order in period 1

üì¶ Shipments Plan - When Orders Actually Arrive

Shipments is when ordered products arrive at your warehouse and become available for use.

  • Timing: Orders arrive after the lead time period
  • Inventory Impact: Available stock increases when shipments arrive
  • Example: Order placed in period 1 with 2-period lead time ‚Üí arrives in period 3

üéØ Why This Distinction Matters

  • üí∞ Cash Flow: You pay when placing orders (procurement), not when they arrive
  • üìä Inventory: You can only use products that have arrived (shipments) to meet demand
  • ‚è∞ Planning: You must order early enough for shipments to arrive when needed

Supply Chain Timeline Concepts:

  • Procurement Plan: When orders are placed to suppliers (considering lead times)
  • Shipments Plan: When orders actually arrive at the warehouse (procurement + lead time)
  • Inventory Levels: Stock levels throughout the planning horizon
  • Demand vs Supply: Comparison of customer demand vs. available supply (shipments)

Model Variables:

  • Products: Each product has a unique ID, name, cost per supplier, shelf life (expiration), and minimum order quantity (MOQ).
  • Suppliers: Each supplier offers certain products and has lead times per product.
  • Demand: Forecasted for each product and period. Assumed to be known and deterministic for this scenario.
  • Inventory: Initial stock, holding cost, warehouse capacity, and safety stock (minimum required inventory at all times) for each product.
  • Logistics Cost: Per-unit and fixed costs for each supplier-product pair.

Constraints & Assumptions:

  • Hard Constraints: All constraints in the model are hard constraints (must be satisfied): demand fulfillment, inventory balance, warehouse capacity, safety stock, shelf life, and MOQ.
  • Soft Constraints: In some advanced scenarios, soft constraints (penalties for violations) can be used for flexibility or robustness, e.g., allowing small backorders or exceeding capacity with a cost penalty.
  • Lead Times: Realistic modeling of time between order placement and order arrival
  • Safety Stock: Minimum inventory required at all times as a buffer against uncertainty
  • MOQ: Minimum order quantities that must be met for each supplier

Assumptions:

  • All demand must be met on time (no backorders allowed).
  • Products do not expire within the planning horizon (shelf life > periods).
  • All suppliers are reliable and always deliver as planned.
  • Costs and demand are deterministic (no uncertainty modeled here).
  • Safety stock is strictly enforced: inventory at end of each period must be at least the safety stock level.
  • Lead times are deterministic and supplier-specific.

Variable Details:

  • product_id: Unique product identifier.
  • supplier_id: Unique supplier identifier.
  • period: Integer time index (e.g., 0, 1, 2, 3, ...).
  • initial_stock: Starting inventory for each product.
  • holding_cost: Cost per unit per period for holding inventory.
  • warehouse_capacity: Maximum inventory allowed for each product.
  • safety_stock: Minimum inventory required for each product at all times (buffer against uncertainty).
  • unit_cost_by_supplier: Cost per unit for each product from each supplier.
  • MOQ: Minimum order quantity for each product.
  • lead_times: Number of periods between order placement and delivery for each product-supplier pair.
  • cost_per_unit: Logistics cost per unit shipped.
  • fixed_cost: Fixed logistics cost per shipment.
